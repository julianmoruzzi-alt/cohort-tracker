🏫 Ofsted Preparation — Gateway Academy Performance Narrative

All figures from IDSR November 2025 (Ofsted's own data). Talking points are contextualised against national averages and the school's specific circumstances.

Opening Statement
Gateway Academy serves a high-mobility community in Tilbury, Thurrock — one of the most deprived areas in England. The school carries a Low Stability flag in both 2024 and 2025 cohorts, meaning headline measures understate true educational impact. Despite this, outcomes are broadly in line with or above national across all key measures.
Disadvantaged Progress
Above — 2 years
Disadvantaged Progress 8 of -0.04 is rated Above national by the IDSR — 0.53 points above the national disadvantaged average of -0.57. Rated Above in both 2022/23 and 2023/24. This is our strongest IDSR banding and demonstrates consistent impact on our most vulnerable learners.
Disadvantaged Attainment
+8.6pp Eng&Maths 5+
34.2% of disadvantaged pupils achieve Grade 5+ in English & Maths vs 25.6% nationally for disadvantaged pupils. Sustained for 3 consecutive years. Disadvantaged A8 of 38.0 is above the national disadvantaged average of 34.9 across all three years.
Attainment 8 — All Pupils
46.0 vs 45.9 nat.
A8 has risen from 44.2 (2022/23) to 46.0 (2024/25) — now above the national average for the first time. Clear upward trajectory over 3 years. Note: "Close to average" IDSR banding is expected given low stability context; the improving trend is the key message.
Progress 8 Recovery
-0.76 → +0.07
P8 has recovered from -0.76 (2021/22, post-COVID) to +0.07 (2023/24) — above the national average of -0.03 in both subsequent years. This recovery reflects sustained improvement in curriculum quality and teaching.
Eng & Maths 5+ — All
47.7% vs 45.2%
47.7% of all pupils achieved Grade 5+ in English & Maths — 2.5pp above the national average of 45.2%. Rising from 44.2% in 2022/23. This is a strong position for a school with 40.6% FSM6 and a Low Stability flag.
Context — Mobility
Low Stability
The Low Stability designation for both 2024 and 2025 cohorts is a significant contextual factor. High pupil mobility means the school receives pupils mid-KS4 who count in results but have not received a full Gateway education. Ofsted guidance acknowledges this as a mitigating factor.
Disadvantaged Gap — Honest
-12.3 A8 gap
A gap of -12.3 A8 points exists between our disadvantaged pupils (38.0) and national non-disadvantaged pupils (50.3). This is acknowledged and is the focus of our Pupil Premium strategy. Our disadvantaged pupils outperform the national disadvantaged average — showing positive impact — but closing the gap to non-disadvantaged nationally is a medium-term goal.

Why this is low: EBacc requires pupils to study a language, history or geography, and a science at GCSE. With 25.1% EAL pupils — many acquiring English as well as their GCSE subjects — and a high proportion of disadvantaged pupils for whom curriculum breadth can be harder to sustain, Gateway's curriculum decisions reflect these contextual realities. The EBacc component A8 score of 12.7 is close to the national 13.4, suggesting that where pupils do enter EBacc subjects, outcomes are comparable to national.

What to say to Ofsted: Be transparent — acknowledge the low entry rate, explain the curriculum rationale in the context of the school's profile, and reference the strong Eng & Maths 5+ performance as evidence of effective core academic outcomes. Note that the government's own 75% EBacc target was not met nationally (35.4%), making this a sector-wide challenge rather than a school-specific failure.
